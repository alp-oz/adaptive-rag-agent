ECB Supervisory Review and Evaluation Process (SREP) — Methodology Overview
============================================================================
Source: European Central Bank Banking Supervision. Synthetic summary for
educational and portfolio demonstration purposes.

OVERVIEW
--------
The Supervisory Review and Evaluation Process (SREP) is the annual cycle
through which the ECB assesses the risks faced by significant institutions
(SIs) under its direct supervision within the Single Supervisory Mechanism
(SSM). The SREP results in binding capital and liquidity requirements above
the Pillar 1 minimum, as well as qualitative supervisory measures.

The ECB directly supervises approximately 113 significant institutions,
including all major eurozone banks. Swedbank's euro area operations fall
under national competent authority supervision (Finansinspektionen) with ECB
involvement through the Joint Supervisory Team (JST) framework.

SREP COMPONENTS
---------------
The SREP assessment covers four main elements:

1. Business Model Analysis (BMA):
   Assesses the viability and sustainability of the bank's business model
   over a 12-month and 3-year forward-looking horizon. Scores from 1 (no
   risk) to 4 (high risk). A score of 4 triggers immediate supervisory
   escalation. Typical areas: revenue concentration, profitability drivers,
   strategic plan credibility, competitive position.

2. Internal Governance and Risk Management:
   Evaluates the adequacy of the bank's governance arrangements, risk
   culture, internal controls, and risk management frameworks. Covers:
   - Board composition and effectiveness
   - Risk appetite framework
   - Internal audit and compliance functions
   - Data quality and risk data aggregation (BCBS 239)
   - Remuneration policies

3. Risk Assessment (capital risks):
   Individual assessment of each material risk type:
   a) Credit and counterparty risk
   b) Market risk (trading book and banking book)
   c) Operational risk (including cyber and conduct risk)
   d) Interest rate risk in the banking book (IRRBB)
   e) Liquidity and funding risk
   f) Concentration risk (sectoral, geographic, single-name)
   g) Profitability and sustainability risk

4. Capital and Liquidity Adequacy Assessment:
   Determines whether the bank holds sufficient capital and liquidity
   given the identified risks. Feeds directly into P2R and P2G setting.

SREP CAPITAL ASSESSMENT
-----------------------
The SREP capital assessment results in three layers of requirements:

Pillar 2 Requirement (P2R):
   - Mandatory, legally binding additional capital above Pillar 1
   - Covers risks underestimated or not covered by Pillar 1
   - Must be met at all times; breach triggers automatic restrictions
   - Communicated to the bank but not publicly disclosed individually
   - ECB average P2R across SSM banks: approximately 2.1% CET1
   - Composition: at least 56.25% must be CET1 (remainder AT1 and T2)

Pillar 2 Guidance (P2G):
   - Non-binding supervisory expectation above P2R
   - Based on adverse stress test scenario capital depletion
   - Banks are expected (but not legally required) to meet P2G
   - Breach of P2G triggers supervisory dialogue, not automatic restrictions
   - ECB average P2G: approximately 1.5% CET1

Combined Buffer Requirement (CBR):
   - Capital Conservation Buffer + Countercyclical Buffer + G-SIB/D-SIB buffer
   - Breach triggers restrictions on distributions (dividends, AT1 coupons,
     variable remuneration)
   - Maximum Distributable Amount (MDA) applies when CBR is breached

TOTAL SREP CAPITAL REQUIREMENT (TSCR) AND OVERALL CAPITAL REQUIREMENT (OCR):
   TSCR = Pillar 1 (8%) + P2R
   OCR  = TSCR + Combined Buffer Requirement

HOW STRESS TEST RESULTS FEED INTO SREP
---------------------------------------
The EBA EU-wide stress test results are a key quantitative input to the
SREP P2G calibration:

1. Capital depletion in the adverse scenario is used to set P2G:
   P2G ≈ Maximum capital depletion in adverse scenario
   (with adjustments for model uncertainty and bank-specific factors)

2. Banks that show large capital depletion in the stress test receive
   higher P2G, effectively requiring them to hold more capital as a buffer
   against a future adverse scenario.

3. The stress test is therefore not a pass/fail exercise — there is no
   minimum threshold below which a bank "fails." Instead, results translate
   directly into forward-looking capital requirements.

4. Qualitative findings from the stress test (data quality, model limitations,
   governance of stress testing) also feed into the governance component of
   SREP scoring.

Example: A bank that depletes 4.5 percentage points of CET1 in the adverse
scenario would typically receive a P2G of approximately 4.0–5.0%, depending
on the supervisory assessment of model risk and management actions.

SREP LIQUIDITY ASSESSMENT
--------------------------
Alongside capital, SREP includes a full liquidity assessment:

Pillar 2 Liquidity Requirement (P2R-L):
   - Additional liquidity above LCR/NSFR minimums
   - May require additional High-Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA)
   - Covers risks not captured by standard ratios: intraday liquidity,
     currency mismatches, franchise-specific deposit flight assumptions

SREP SCORES AND OVERALL ASSESSMENT
------------------------------------
Each SREP component receives a score from 1 to 4:
   1 = No discernible risk
   2 = Low risk
   3 = Medium risk
   4 = High risk

The overall SREP score is a composite of all component scores. It determines
the intensity of ongoing supervision:
   Score 1-2: Standard supervision
   Score 3:   Enhanced supervision, possible on-site inspections
   Score 4:   Intensive supervision, potential early intervention measures

ECB publishes aggregate SREP statistics annually showing distribution of
scores across SSM banks. In 2023, approximately 70% of banks received an
overall SREP score of 2 or 3.

SREP IN THE CONTEXT OF THE 2023 STRESS TEST
--------------------------------------------
Following the 2023 EBA stress test, the ECB used results to:

1. Set P2G for 2024 SREP cycle based on adverse scenario capital depletion
   (average depletion of 4.6 percentage points across EU banks)

2. Identify banks with disproportionate CRE exposures for targeted
   deep-dive supervisory reviews

3. Launch thematic review of IRRBB management across SSM banks, given the
   sharp interest rate shock in the adverse scenario

4. Assess preparedness for the output floor implementation under Basel III
   final rules, with particular focus on IRB model recalibration needs

SUPERVISORY MEASURES
--------------------
Following SREP, the ECB may impose:
   - Capital add-ons (P2R increases)
   - Specific own funds requirements for individual risk categories
   - Liquidity add-ons
   - Qualitative measures: remediation plans, management body changes,
     risk appetite revisions, model reviews
   - In severe cases: early intervention measures under BRRD
     (Bank Recovery and Resolution Directive)
