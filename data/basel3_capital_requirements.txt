Basel III Capital Requirements Framework
========================================
Source: Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS), BIS. Synthetic summary
for educational and portfolio demonstration purposes.

OVERVIEW
--------
Basel III is the international regulatory framework for banks developed by the
Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS) in response to the 2007–2009
global financial crisis. It strengthens bank capital requirements by increasing
bank liquidity and decreasing bank leverage. The framework was phased in from
2013 and fully implemented by January 2023.

CAPITAL TIERS
-------------
Bank capital under Basel III is divided into three tiers:

Tier 1 Capital (going-concern capital):
  Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1):
    - Ordinary shares and share premium accounts
    - Retained earnings and accumulated other comprehensive income
    - Other disclosed reserves
    - Common shares issued by consolidated subsidiaries
    CET1 is the highest quality capital — it absorbs losses while the bank
    continues operating. Minimum requirement: 4.5% of risk-weighted assets.

  Additional Tier 1 (AT1):
    - Instruments that are subordinated, have fully discretionary non-
      cumulative dividends/coupons, and have no maturity date
    - Contingent convertible bonds (CoCos) that convert to equity or are
      written down when CET1 falls below a trigger (typically 5.125%)
    Minimum Tier 1 capital (CET1 + AT1): 6.0% of risk-weighted assets.

Tier 2 Capital (gone-concern capital):
    - Subordinated debt with minimum 5-year original maturity
    - General loan loss provisions (up to 1.25% of credit RWA)
    - Instruments issued by consolidated subsidiaries
    Minimum Total Capital (Tier 1 + Tier 2): 8.0% of risk-weighted assets.

CAPITAL BUFFERS
---------------
On top of minimum requirements, Basel III introduced mandatory and
discretionary capital buffers, all of which must be met with CET1:

Capital Conservation Buffer (CCB):
    - 2.5% of RWA, mandatory for all banks
    - Restricts distributions (dividends, buybacks, bonuses) when breached
    - Effective minimum CET1 with CCB: 7.0%

Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB):
    - 0% to 2.5% of RWA, set by national authorities
    - Activated when credit growth is excessive; released in downturns
    - Swedish Riksbank set CCyB at 2.0% prior to COVID-19 pandemic, then
      released it to 0% in March 2020, subsequently rebuilding to 2.0%

G-SIB Buffer (Global Systemically Important Banks):
    - 1.0% to 3.5% of RWA, depending on systemic importance score
    - Applied to ~30 global banks (e.g., BNP Paribas, Deutsche Bank, ING)
    - No Swedish bank currently holds G-SIB designation

D-SIB Buffer (Domestic Systemically Important Banks):
    - 1.0% to 3.0% of RWA, set by national authorities
    - In Sweden: Swedbank, SEB, Handelsbanken, and Nordea each carry a
      D-SIB surcharge of 2.0%, reflecting their systemic importance to the
      Swedish financial system

Systemic Risk Buffer:
    - Applied in some EU jurisdictions (Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland)
    - Sweden applies a 3.0% systemic risk buffer to Swedish exposures of
      the four major banks, reflecting real estate concentration risk

COMBINED CAPITAL REQUIREMENTS (SWEDISH MAJOR BANKS)
----------------------------------------------------
For Swedbank, SEB, Handelsbanken, and Nordea operating in Sweden:
    CET1 minimum (Pillar 1):              4.5%
    Capital Conservation Buffer:          2.5%
    Countercyclical Capital Buffer:       2.0%
    D-SIB Buffer:                         2.0%
    Systemic Risk Buffer:                 3.0%
    ----------------------------------------
    Combined CET1 requirement:           ~14.0%
    (exact figure varies by bank and SREP outcome)

These requirements are among the highest in the EU, reflecting the Swedish
financial supervisory authority's (Finansinspektionen) conservative approach.

RISK-WEIGHTED ASSETS (RWA)
--------------------------
Capital ratios are expressed as a percentage of risk-weighted assets (RWA),
not total assets. RWA reflects the riskiness of each asset:

Credit risk RWA:
    - Standardised approach: fixed risk weights by asset class (e.g.,
      residential mortgages: 35%, corporate loans: 100%, sovereign: 0–150%)
    - Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) approach: banks use own models to
      estimate probability of default (PD), loss given default (LGD), and
      exposure at default (EAD). Swedish banks use IRB for most portfolios.
    - IRB typically produces lower RWA than standardised, giving Swedish
      banks higher reported CET1 ratios vs. banks on standardised approach

Market risk RWA:
    - Trading book exposures: interest rate risk, equity risk, FX risk
    - Standardised or Internal Models Approach (IMA)
    - Under Basel III's Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB),
      market risk RWA is expected to increase significantly

Operational risk RWA:
    - Business Indicator Component (BIC) under Basel III final rules
    - Replaces the Advanced Measurement Approach (AMA) from Basel II

OUTPUT FLOOR
------------
A key feature of Basel III finalisation (sometimes called "Basel IV"):
    - Banks using internal models cannot produce total RWA below 72.5% of
      the standardised approach RWA (phased in 2025–2030)
    - This "output floor" limits the RWA benefit from IRB models
    - Expected to increase RWA for Swedish banks by 15–25%, as their IRB
      models produce particularly low RWA on mortgage portfolios
    - Finansinspektionen has estimated Swedbank's CET1 ratio would fall by
      approximately 2–3 percentage points when the output floor fully applies

LEVERAGE RATIO
--------------
The leverage ratio is a non-risk-based backstop:
    Tier 1 capital / Total exposure measure ≥ 3.0%

Total exposure includes:
    - On-balance-sheet assets (net of Tier 1 deductions)
    - Derivative exposures (replacement cost + potential future exposure)
    - Securities financing transaction (SFT) exposures
    - Off-balance-sheet items (credit conversion factors apply)

Swedish major banks typically report leverage ratios of 4.5–5.5%, well above
the 3% minimum, reflecting their relatively low derivative and trading book
exposures compared to global investment banks.

LIQUIDITY REQUIREMENTS
----------------------
Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR):
    - High-quality liquid assets (HQLA) / Net cash outflows over 30 days ≥ 100%
    - HQLA includes: central bank reserves, government bonds, covered bonds
    - Swedish banks maintain LCR above 150%, partly reflecting Finansinspektionen's
      requirement to hold LCR above 100% in each major currency

Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR):
    - Available stable funding / Required stable funding ≥ 100%
    - Measures whether long-term assets are funded by stable long-term liabilities
    - Swedish banks NSFR: typically 115–130%, comfortably above minimum

PILLAR 2 REQUIREMENTS
---------------------
Beyond Pillar 1 minimum requirements, supervisors set additional Pillar 2
requirements (P2R) through the SREP process:
    - P2R covers risks not fully captured by Pillar 1: concentration risk,
      interest rate risk in the banking book (IRRBB), pension risk
    - In Sweden, P2R adds approximately 1.5–3.0% CET1 for major banks
    - P2R is bank-specific and confidential, but disclosed in aggregate

CAPITAL DEPLETION UNDER STRESS
-------------------------------
Under EBA stress tests, capital depletion is driven by:
    1. Credit losses: impairments on loans (largest driver, 60–70% of total)
    2. Market risk losses: mark-to-market on trading book and AFS portfolio
    3. Net interest income compression: spread compression under adverse rates
    4. Operational risk losses: conduct charges, IT failures, fraud
    5. RWA inflation: migration of performing loans to higher risk buckets

Banks with high IRB-modelled RWA benefit from lower starting capital
requirements but are exposed to RWA inflation when models recalibrate under
stress, amplifying capital ratio declines.
